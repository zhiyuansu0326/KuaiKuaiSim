 5   1    Introduction
 6   In modern recommender systems, re-ranking is the final decision layer that determines how a small
 7   displayed slate is selected and arranged from a substantially larger upstream candidate pool. Unlike
 8   upstream retrieval or coarse ranking stages, re-ranking is not merely about assigning independent
 9   relevance scores to individual items. Instead, it is a constrained list-level combinatorial decision
10   problem: the system must decide not only which items from the candidate pool should enter the
11   final slate, but also in what order and at which positions they should be exposed. This distinction is
12   especially critical in item recommendation scenarios, where position bias, cross-item interference,
13   platform revenue, and user experience are all jointly mediated by the final page-level arrangement.
14   Prior studies on list-wise recommendation have similarly emphasized that the key difficulty lies
15   in modeling correlations within the exposed list rather than predicting each item independently.
16   In particular, KuaiSim explicitly identifies request-level recommendation as a list-wise problem,
17   where each action corresponds to an ordered list presented to the user, and conventional point-wise
18   evaluation is insufficient for capturing cross-item patterns and list-level effects [6].
19   However, in real-world systems, item re-ranking is not a one-shot static ranking problem. The way
20   exposure is allocated at the current step influences not only immediate responses such as clicks, likes,
21   or conversions, but also the user’s future interest state, session depth, return probability, and long-term
22   engagement. Therefore, item re-ranking is more accurately viewed as a sequential decision-making
23   problem rather than a single-step list optimization problem. This perspective is consistent with recent
24   research on long-term recommendation. KuaiSim distinguishes three levels of recommendation tasks:
25   request-level list-wise recommendation, whole-session sequential recommendation, and cross-session
26   retention optimization, thereby emphasizing that each recommendation reshapes later requests and
27   later user feedback [6]. Likewise, DT4IER argues that optimizing only immediate feedback such as
28   CTR is often insufficient, since such short-term signals may fail to reflect long-term user satisfaction
29   and retention, and practical systems must jointly consider immediate engagement and long-term
30   return behavior [3]. AURO further shows that user retention optimization naturally leads to a dynamic
31   recommendation environment in which user behavior patterns evolve over time, making long-term
32   recommendation a non-stationary sequential control problem [5].
33   Although reinforcement learning has been widely adopted for long-term optimization in recommender
34   systems, most existing methods still do not explicitly rewrite the geometry of the ranking action itself.
35   A common design is to let the policy output a continuous control vector or latent action, and then rely
36   on a pretrained scoring model or a predefined ranking function to produce the final recommendation

38   and uses a continuous action space together with a scoring model and ranking function to optimize
39   user retention under non-stationary dynamics [5]. These approaches are effective for long-term
40   reward optimization, yet the ranking decision remains an indirect consequence of a downstream
41   ranking module, rather than a native structured action. As a result, the item–position assignment that
42   fundamentally defines re-ranking is not treated as the primary decision object.
43   On the other hand, research on structured action learning has shown that permutation matrices provide
44   a natural representation for combinatorial decisions involving matching, ranking, and ordering. In
45   particular, Sinkhorn Policy Gradient (SPG) demonstrates that actor–critic learning can be extended
46   to permutation-valued actions by introducing a Sinkhorn layer that outputs a continuous relaxation
47   of permutation matrices [1]. Relatedly, Gumbel-Sinkhorn networks show that a score matrix can
48   be perturbed by Gumbel noise, relaxed by Sinkhorn scaling, and rounded to a discrete matching
49   with the Hungarian algorithm, yielding a reparameterized sampling view for latent permutations [4].
50   Importantly, however, SPG is formulated in an average-reward contextual bandit setting, and the paper
51   explicitly notes that its derivation can be viewed as a one-step reinforcement learning formulation
52   obtained by setting the episode horizon to one; extending the method to episodic RL is left as
53   future work [1]. In other words, existing structured-action methods clarify how permutation-valued
54   decisions can be relaxed and sampled, but they do not address the defining challenge of sequential
55   re-ranking: the ranking action is not merely a permutation output for a static instance, but a control
56   variable that shapes future user states through exposure.
57   This leaves a clear gap in the literature. Existing long-term recommendation methods model sequential
58   optimization but usually do not represent ranking actions in an assignment-native way. Existing
59   permutation-based actor–critic methods show that structured combinatorial actions are learnable, but
60   remain in contextual bandit or one-step settings rather than sequential re-ranking environments with
61   evolving user preferences. Consequently, an important question remains unresolved: when a ranking
62   action should itself be treated as a structured display-or-discard decision, and when that decision
63   continuously affects the future user-interest distribution, how should we optimize it under a unified
64   reinforcement learning framework?
65   In this work, we argue that the action in item re-ranking should not be viewed as a set of independent
66   item scores, nor merely as an auxiliary input to a downstream ranking function. Instead, it should
67   be modeled directly as an item–position allocation plan on the final reranking shortlist. Because the
68   shortlist size K is larger than the number of exposed positions N , the action must simultaneously
69   decide which N items are displayed and which K − N items are discarded. Following the transport-
70   plan notation common in differentiable top-k formulations, we represent each re-ranking action as a
71   rectangular partial-assignment matrix whose rows index shortlisted items and whose columns index
72   the N display positions plus one dump slot. The policy therefore outputs a score matrix whose
73   Sinkhorn relaxation defines a soft transport plan, while the environment-facing discrete action is
74   sampled by Gumbel perturbation followed by Sinkhorn relaxation and Hungarian rounding. This
75   representation preserves the combinatorial structure of reranking while bringing display competition,
76   position effects, and discard decisions into the action space itself rather than leaving them to post-
77   processing.
78   Building on this action formulation, we further cast sequential re-ranking as an actor–critic problem
79   over rectangular transport-style ranking actions. Moreover, motivated by the geometric structure
80   induced by Sinkhorn scaling with prescribed marginals, we introduce a maximum matrix-entropy
81   regularization principle to stabilize exploration and optimization in this structured action space.
82   The resulting framework unifies three ingredients that have previously remained largely separate:
83   assignment-native action modeling for ranking, long-term user optimization with evolving request
84   histories, and entropy-regularized control tailored to partial-assignment matrices. Rather than merely
85   reusing Sinkhorn within reinforcement learning, our formulation reinterprets re-ranking itself as a
86   structured control problem on a ranking transport polytope.

104   3      Matrix Entropy Reranking Actor-Critic
105   Our method, Matrix Entropy Reranking Actor-Critic (MERA), is organized from structured action
106   geometry to learning. We first represent reranking as constrained transport-style partial assignment,
107   then introduce a compact actor–critic interface, and finally derive a practical hard-action training
108   procedure.

116   3.2      Actor and Critic Interfaces

117   The actor defines a parameterized policy πϕ (· | Ot ): given request Ot , it outputs a soft matrix
118   Pϕ (Ot ) ∈ TK,N as an action-distribution representation, and a sampler S draws a hard action by
119   Xt = S(Pϕ (Ot )) ∈ XK,N .
120   The critic evaluates how good a realized action is for the current request. It takes (Ot , Xt ) as input
121   and outputs Qθ (Ot , Xt ), which is trained to match the regularized expected return. Detailed network
122   parameterization is deferred to Appendix A.1.
